From Burnout to Building

Why I am moving back into engineering

Published on May 27, 2026

This is not a dramatic reinvention. It is a return to the part of the work that made sense to me in the first place.

I have been a system builder for most of my life. That is what pulled me toward IT in the first place: the possibility of taking a messy problem, breaking it into parts, shaping those parts into a working structure, and then seeing the whole thing come alive. Not just talking about the system. Not only coordinating the system. Building it.

My career did not follow a straight line. I worked in QA Automation, then moved deeper into leadership, project management, delivery, and team coordination. Those roles taught me a lot. I learned how teams break, how delivery systems rot, how communication fails, how stakeholders misunderstand incentives, and how technical debt quietly turns into organizational debt. I also learned how much good management can help when it is done with care.

But there was a cost. Over time, I drifted away from hands-on engineering. At first it felt reasonable: the team needed coordination, the client needed clarity, the process needed someone to hold it together. Then the gap grew. I was still close to technical work, but no longer close enough to the act of building. And slowly, something important disappeared.

What Burnout Taught Me

Burnout is not always caused by laziness, weakness, or a lack of resilience. Sometimes it is caused by spending too much time in a shape that no longer fits you. For me, the issue was not that project management was useless. It was not. The issue was that my strongest source of energy has always come from building systems, and I had moved too far from that source.

There is a specific kind of exhaustion that appears when your responsibility grows but your creative agency shrinks. You become accountable for outcomes, dependencies, estimates, expectations, delays, politics, and morale, but the actual act of making something useful is always one step away. You are close enough to the work to understand what is happening, but not close enough to change the system directly through code, architecture, or implementation.

That gap is dangerous. It creates a strange kind of professional dissociation. You can still perform. You can still hold meetings. You can still produce reports. You can still look responsible. But inside, the work starts feeling indirect. You are moving information around instead of shaping reality.

Taking a step back made the conclusion obvious: I want to build again. Not as a hobby squeezed into exhausted evenings. Not as a nostalgic escape. As the center of my professional direction.

Why Move Back Into a Technical Role?

The reason is simple, but not shallow: engineering gives me direct contact with consequences. A design works or it does not. A service starts or it crashes. An API contract is clear or it becomes a future migration nightmare. A boundary is well-defined or it leaks complexity everywhere. Engineering forces decisions to become concrete.

That concreteness matters to me. It is where abstract thinking becomes useful. Project management taught me how to see systems at the human and organizational level. QA Automation taught me how to think in terms of verification, failure paths, and repeatability. Moving back into engineering is not a rejection of those years. It is the attempt to combine them.

I do not want to become a developer who only writes tickets into code. I want to become a better system designer. My long-term goal is software architecture, and for that, management experience is not wasted. It gives me a different lens: architecture is not only about services, databases, queues, and APIs. It is also about constraints, ownership, operational reality, communication, risk, and the ability of a team to safely evolve a system over time.

But to earn that role properly, I need to rebuild technical depth. Architecture without implementation experience becomes theater. Diagrams without code become decoration. Strategy without hands-on feedback becomes guesswork. So the path is clear: build real projects, document the tradeoffs, expose the decisions, and let the work speak.

Why Engineering?

Engineering is not just coding. Coding is the visible part. Engineering is the discipline of making systems reliable enough to be trusted, understandable enough to be changed, and constrained enough to survive real use.

That is what attracts me. I like the space between idea and implementation. I like the question: What is the simplest system that can solve this problem without lying about its limitations? I like designing boundaries. I like deciding where complexity should live. I like thinking about the boring things that decide whether a project survives: configuration, observability, data flow, failure modes, security, testing, deployment, and maintainability.

This is also why I am interested in local-first AI systems and developer tooling. They sit at the intersection of several things I care about: software architecture, automation, privacy, human judgment, and practical productivity. They are technical enough to be meaningful, but close enough to real human workflows to matter.

The First Experiment: ALT-Repo

ALT-Repo is a small, backend-only experiment: a local-first AI code review assistant built around Gitea, Docker, FastAPI, and a locally running LLM through LM Studio. The idea is intentionally simple. A pull request is opened or updated in Gitea. Gitea sends a webhook to the reviewer service. The service fetches the pull request diff, prepares it, sends it to a local OpenAI-compatible model endpoint, and posts the generated review back to the pull request.

The important part is not that the model gives perfect review feedback. It does not. The important part is the shape of the system: code stays local, the workflow is understandable, the integration is small, and the AI is placed inside a narrow, auditable boundary. It is not a magic agent wandering through the repository. It is a constrained tool in a specific workflow.

Architecturally, the MVP is deliberately modest. FastAPI receives the webhook. A review pipeline fetches the diff from Gitea. The diff is lightly sanitized and truncated. A prompt template is filled. A local LLM generates markdown feedback. The result is posted back as a pull request comment. The core modules are separated by responsibility: configuration, webhook handling, Gitea API access, LLM access, diff utilities, prompt building, and the review service itself.

That simplicity is a feature. An MVP should make the main idea visible. The current system is not a production-grade reviewer. It still has trust-boundary and operational risks to address: local-only endpoint validation needs to be stricter, webhook signature handling should not silently become optional in serious deployments, and runtime dependencies such as prompt templates should be validated earlier. These are not reasons to hide the project. They are the exact kind of tradeoffs worth documenting.

My Stance on AI

I am not anti-AI. I am also not interested in worshipping it. My stance is simple: AI is a tool, not an authority.

The current hype cycle often treats AI as if it can replace judgment. I do not believe that is a healthy or technically mature position. AI can draft, summarize, classify, inspect, compare, and suggest. It can help us move faster through certain kinds of work. But it does not remove accountability. It does not understand business consequences the way a responsible human does. It does not own the system. It does not carry the operational burden when something breaks.

That is why I prefer constrained AI over vague autonomy. A local code review assistant is useful because it has a clear job: look at a diff and produce feedback. A documentation assistant can be useful because it has a bounded context: read this module and explain how it fits into the system. A learning tutor can be useful because it has an educational goal and can be evaluated against student progress. The more specific the boundary, the more useful AI becomes.

The opposite approach is dangerous: vague agents with broad permissions, unclear objectives, hidden reasoning, weak auditability, and too much trust. That is not engineering. That is gambling with extra steps.

Local-First AI Is Not Just a Preference

I care about local-first AI because privacy and control matter. Developer workflows often involve proprietary code, internal architecture, business logic, credentials, private tickets, and sensitive operational context. Sending all of that to external cloud services should not be the default assumption.

Local-first does not mean cloud is always wrong. It means the default architecture should respect data boundaries. If a team chooses to use a cloud model, that should be a deliberate decision with clear tradeoffs, not an accidental consequence of convenience. Local models are not always better. They can be slower, weaker, harder to operate, and more limited. But they also give us something important: ownership of the execution environment.

For developer tooling, this matters. The future I am interested in is not one where AI replaces engineers. It is one where engineers build better tools around AI: tools that are observable, configurable, secure, modular, and honest about uncertainty.

AI-Assisted Review Is a Safety Net, Not a Gatekeeper

Code review is a useful example because it shows both the promise and the danger of AI. A model can notice suspicious patterns, missing tests, unclear error handling, risky configuration defaults, or documentation drift. It can be a second pair of eyes. It can help junior developers learn. It can reduce the chance that obvious issues slip through when everyone is tired.

But it should not become the final authority. Human review still matters because humans understand intent, product context, team history, deployment risk, and the cost of being wrong. AI can produce confident nonsense. It can miss important context. It can over-focus on style and under-focus on business risk. It can be useful and unreliable at the same time.

That is why my preferred model is not AI replaces review. It is AI prepares review. It highlights possibilities. It asks questions. It points to risks. Then a human decides what matters.

How This Connects to ALT

ALT-Repo is not the final destination. It is one experiment in a broader direction: local-first AI systems, developer tooling, and eventually ALT — Artificial Learning Tutor.

The connection is not only technical. It is philosophical. ALT, as I imagine it, should not be another magical black box that pretends to teach. It should be a learning system with explicit boundaries, teacher-provided context, measurable progress, modular skills, and transparent behavior. It should help people learn without replacing the responsibility of the learner or the teacher.

That requires the same engineering stance: clear architecture, local or controlled data handling, constrained AI behavior, meaningful feedback loops, and enough humility to admit when the system does not know.

Building in Public

This channel and this blog are part of the same experiment. I am documenting the move back into engineering in public: the projects, the architecture, the implementation choices, the mistakes, the refactoring, the tradeoffs, and the lessons.

I do not want to present a fake story where everything is polished from the beginning. Real engineering is iterative. The first version is often rough. The first architecture is often incomplete. The first assumptions often break. That is fine, as long as the work keeps moving and the lessons are captured.

In a strange way, this is also a recovery process. Burnout narrowed my world. Building opens it again. Every working service, every fixed bug, every clarified boundary, every small architectural decision is a step back toward the version of the field that made me care in the first place.

What I Am Optimizing For

I am not optimizing for hype. I am not optimizing for pretending that every AI demo is a revolution. I am optimizing for engineering substance.

  • Local-first when sensitive context is involved.
  • Human judgment as the standard.
  • AI as an assistant, not an owner.
  • Architecture that makes tradeoffs visible.
  • Tools that solve real workflow problems.
  • Projects that can teach both me and others something useful.

That is the direction: rebuild technical depth, produce useful projects, document the journey, and move toward software architecture through actual engineering work rather than slogans.

Final Thoughts

Moving back into a technical role is not a rejection of project management. It is a correction of course. The years spent in delivery taught me how systems fail at the human level. Now I want to combine that with the engineering depth needed to build systems that fail less often, recover more clearly, and remain understandable as they grow.

AI will be part of that journey, but not as magic. Not as a replacement for thinking. Not as a substitute for responsibility.

AI as a tool. Human judgment as the standard. Engineering as the craft.
