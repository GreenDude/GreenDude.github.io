Data Driven Scrum

Published on September 17, 2025

SCRUM — a framework loved by some and loathed by many. Let’s be honest, most of the hate comes from developers. But why? Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland were developers themselves. They created Scrum to solve the very organizational problems they faced. Surely it was meant to make life easier for developers?

In principle, yes. Unfortunately, in practice, Scrum often suffers because the role of the Scrum Master is filled by someone who doesn’t fully understand the work. The result? Things don’t go as smoothly as they should.

Scrum rests on three pillars: Transparency, Inspection, and Adaptation. When used correctly, they keep teams healthy and productive. But in reality, I’ve seen these pillars ignored or applied half-heartedly. Retrospectives become a formality with no real follow-up. Daily stand-ups turn into status reports for the Scrum Master rather than coordination tools for the team. And teams are pushed to deliver “above and beyond” at the expense of sustainability.

This culture breeds resentment. Developers don’t just dislike the process — they begin to hate Scrum itself. Over the years, I’ve seen many pitfalls Scrum Masters fall into (including a few I’ve stumbled into myself). But today, I want to focus on one specific piece that often gets neglected or misunderstood: internal reporting.

Transparency: Using Reporting to See Clearly

When I first entered project management, I saw reporting as nothing more than a way to update clients or management. That’s how most people treat it. Luckily, early in my career, I crossed paths with Ana Vintila, a brilliant Agile Coach who showed me what reporting could really do.

When I became a Scrum Master, I inherited a fantastic reporting structure. We tracked Delivery KPIs, Team Health KPIs, and Narrative Data — a combination that gave us a 360° view of the team’s performance and morale.

For example, in one project, sprints 55 to 63 showed a consistent drop across all KPIs. From sprint 64 onwards, things improved. What changed? Building a Reporting Framework

For each project, the reporting structure must be tailored. I use three categories:

  1. Delivery KPIs – Metrics like velocity, spillover (unfinished story points), and capacity.

  2. Team Health KPIs – Planning confidence, happiness (sometimes split into satisfaction with the team, the project, and cross-team collaboration).

  3. Narrative Data – Brief but descriptive notes from retrospectives that explain context behind the numbers.

Consistently capturing accurate, relevant data enables Transparency — the first pillar of Scrum. But raw numbers aren’t enough. You need to make them visible:

  • Apply conditional formatting.

  • Use charts or even simple arrows to highlight trends.

  • Present the data so it can be understood at a glance.

Inspect: Finding the Patterns

Once the data is clear, the next pillar — Inspection — comes into play. Here’s my approach:

  1. Correlate delivery metrics – Compare capacity and velocity to see if the team is delivering as much as it can realistically handle.

  2. Assess spillover trends – Look for recurring unfinished work and link it to planning confidence or dependencies.

  3. Factor in team health – Declining morale or confidence can signal burnout or poor planning.

  4. Use narrative data for context – Numbers show what happened; narrative data explains why.

Look for cause-and-effect patterns. Example: The team’s dev lead left, which reduced technical capacity, which lowered morale, which then reduced velocity.

Finally, turn findings into actionable advice — practical steps the team can take to address problems.

Adapt: Turning Insight into Action

Data without action is useless. Adaptation means using insights to make targeted, incremental changes:

  • Low velocity vs. high capacity → Remove blockers or adjust workload.

  • High spillover → Improve backlog refinement or commit to less work.

  • Low planning confidence → Strengthen estimation and preparation.

  • Dropping happiness → Address workload balance, team autonomy, or interpersonal issues.

Changes should be small, measurable, and tested for a sprint. Review metrics afterward to confirm the impact. Involve the whole team — adaptation works best when everyone owns it. This creates a feedback loop: Transparency → Inspect → Adapt → Repeat.

AI as a Scrum Ally

Let’s face it — maintaining this level of reporting and analysis is tedious. This is where AI can help. Not replace humans (that’s impossible in Scrum), but assist in processing large data sets and spotting patterns.

One method I use is the CREATE formula for prompt engineering, especially when anonymizing sensitive data with placeholders like [CLIENT_ORGANIZATION] or [John Doe].

  • Character – Define who the AI should act as (e.g., a project manager analyzing KPIs).

  • Request – State exactly what you want (e.g., analyze KPI trends and give actionable recommendations).

  • Example – Show the AI the output format and style you expect.

  • Adjust – Correct the AI when it goes off track.

  • Type – Specify the exact output format (bullets, tables, narrative).

  • Evaluate – Always check the AI’s work for accuracy.

AI can save time, but it can also hallucinate. Always review results critically and ensure stakeholders agree on its use.

Final Thought

Most Scrum Masters don’t apply this method because it’s effort-heavy. But when done right, internal reporting creates a foundation for real transparency, sharp inspection, and meaningful adaptation. Add AI as a helper — not a crutch — and you can turn raw numbers into the kind of insight that keeps teams healthy, productive, and maybe even… a little more in love with Scrum.
